Recommendations

         

               

                  

                     

                        
Urgent considerations

                        
    
                           
  • Assess for signs of shock or hypotension (ABCDE approach) as this can indicate a massive/central PE with high risk of a poor outcome.
     
                           
      
                           
    • Seek senior support and escalate to critical care if a high-risk PE is suspected. [ESC][ACP]
      • 
                           
    • A high-risk PE is defined as a systolic blood pressure (SBP) <90 mmHg or an SBP drop ≥ 40 mmHg for >15 minutes, if not caused by a new-onset arrhythmia, hypovolaemia, or sepsis. [ESC]
      • 
                           
    • Other signs of shock include altered cognition, cool extremities, mottled or ashen skin, slow capillary refill, clamminess, and oliguria.
      • 
                           
    • If the patient is in peri-arrest/cardiac arrest and PE is suspected it is common practice to give thrombolysis without waiting for results of investigations, based on discussion between senior clinicians.
      • 
                           
    • Ensure early respiratory support. Titrate oxygen to 94% to 98% (or 88% to 92% for patients at risk of hypercapnic failure). [ESC]
      • 
                           
    • Give intravenous fluids if SBP < 90 mmHg or there are other signs of shock but monitor for signs of heart failure. [ESC]
      • 
                           
    
                           
    • 
                           
  • In a patient with shock or hypotension, urgently confirm or rule out a PE using either computed tomographic pulmonary angiography (CTPA) or echocardiography (if CTPA is unavailable or the patient’s condition is so critical that only bedside investigations are possible). [ESC]
                           
      
                           
    • If PE is confirmed on CTPA or signs of right ventricular (RV) overload are seen on echocardiography, arrange urgent primary reperfusion, usually with thrombolysis.
      • 
                           
    
                           
    • 
                           

                     

                  

               

   
               
Key points

                  

                     

                              
                              

                                 

                                 
Presentation

                                 
    
                                    
  • Suspect pulmonary embolism (PE) in a patient with acute onset of dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, or features of deep vein thrombosis (DVT).
                                    
      
                                    
    • At least one of these features is present in 97% of people with PE.[MISS]
      • 
                                    
    • Symptoms typically come on suddenly rather than gradually.
      • 
                                    
    • Dyspnoea is the most common presenting feature and may be acute and severe in a central PE but is often mild and transient in a small, peripheral PE.
      • 
                                    
    
                                    
    • 
                                    
  • Around 95% of patients with PE are haemodynamically stable on presentation.
                                       
      
                                    
    • The 5% of patients who have a high-risk (haemodynamically unstable) PE may also have features of hypotension, shock, or tachycardia and can be hypoxaemic at rest.
      • 
                                    
    • 
                                    
                                    
  • A patient with a PE may also present with cough or, more rarely, with fever or haemoptysis. [ESC]
    • 
                                    
  • Strong risk factors include pregnancy/6 weeks postpartum; active cancer; recent surgery or immobilisation; and a history of DVT. [ESC]
    • 
                                 

                              

                           
                              

                              

                              
Assessing the clinical probability of PE

                              
    
                                 
  • Use the Wells (or Geneva) score to assess the likelihood of a PE in any patient who is haemodynamically stable. This will categorise the patient into either ‘PE likely’ (Wells score >4) or 'PE unlikely' (Wells score ≤4).[ESC] [NICE] 
    • 
                                 
  • PE can be ruled out in around 30% of patients who present to the emergency department with a suspected PE through a combination of clinical probability assessment using the Wells (or Geneva) score, plus D-dimer testing if indicated. [ESC]
    • 
                                 
  • If a patient has a ‘PE likely’ Wells (or Geneva) score, start anticoagulation then arrange investigation with definitive imaging (computed tomographic pulmonary angiography [CTPA] if available).
    • 
                                 
  • If a patient has a 'PE unlikely' Wells (or Geneva) score, order a D-dimer unless the patient fulfils all the Pulmonary Embolism Rule-Out Criteria (in which case a PE can be effectively ruled out and no further investigations are indicated). [NICE] [ESC] [ACP]
    • 
                                 
  • If D-dimer level is elevated, start anticoagulation and arrange CTPA to confirm or exclude a PE. A normal D-dimer level safely excludes a PE in a patient who had a 'PE unlikely' Wells (or Geneva) pre-test score.
    • 
                                 
  • Other differentials to consider include respiratory, cardiac, and musculoskeletal causes as well as gastro-oesophageal reflux disease and pregnancy. Other causes of syncope or pre-syncope should also be examined including vasovagal syncope, orthostatic (postural) hypotension, cardiac arrhythmias, seizures, and cerebrovascular disorders. [ESC]
    • 
                              

                              

                           
                              

                                 

                                 
Diagnostic confirmation

                                 
    
                                    
  • Confirm diagnosis in most patients using CTPA, which will show direct visualisation of a thrombus in a pulmonary artery and appears as a partial or complete intraluminal filling defect. [ESC] [NICE][ACP]
    • 
                                    
  • Avoid CTPA radiation in patients with a suspected concurrent DVT by using lower limb compression venous ultrasound. [NICE] [ACP]
    • 
                                    
  • A ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) scan may be preferred to CTPA to minimise radiation exposure in certain patient groups. This is a radiation- and contrast medium-sparing procedure and should be used in patients with contraindications or relative contraindications to CT (e.g., contrast allergy, moderate to severe renal failure, pregnancy, young patients).[ESC] [NICE]
    • 
                                 

                              
         
                              
                              

                                 

                                 
Suspected PE in pregnancy

                                 
    
                                    
  • PE is the leading cause of maternal mortality in developed countries. There is a fourfold increased risk of venous thromboembolism (VTE) throughout pregnancy compared with non-pregnant women. This risk rises sharply in the postnatal period and is up to 60 times higher in the 3 months after delivery, compared with the risk in non-pregnant women. [ESC] [TOWER]
    • 
                                    
  • Symptoms and signs of PE may be less specific in pregnant women. [ACP] Be cautious in interpreting breathlessness as it is a common symptom in pregnancy. [ESC]
    • 
                                    
  • Investigation of PE in pregnancy should aim to minimise exposure to radiation-associated imaging wherever possible. [ESC]
    •

Clinical presentation

                                 

                              

                              

                                 
Symptoms of PE typically have an acute rather than a gradual onset [ESC].

                                 
Patients typically present with at least one of dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, or features of deep vein thrombosis (DVT). [ESC] [NICE]

                                 
    
                                 
  • Dyspnoea
                                 
      
                                 
    • Themost common presenting feature - it is present in 50% of patientswho are subsequently found to have a PE. [ESC]
      • 
                                 
    • Dyspnoea may be acute and severe in a central PE but is often mild and may be transient in a small peripheral PE. [ESC]
      • 
                                 
    • Tachypnoea (respiratory rate ≥20 breaths per minute) is a common presenting sign in patients with acute PE (21%-39%). [POLLACK]
      • 
                                 
    
                                 
    • 
                                 

   
                                 

                                    

                                    
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                    
In a patient with pre-existing heart failure or lung disease, worsening dyspnoea may be the only presenting symptom of a PE.[ESC]

                                    

                                    

                                    
Pleuritic chest pain

   
                                    
    
                                    
  • Seen at presentation in 39% of patientssubsequently diagnosed with a PE. [ESC]
    • 
                                    
  • The pain is normally localised to one side of the chest and is unlikely to be central. This is usually caused by pleural irritation due to distal emboli causing pulmonary infarction. [STEIN]
    • 
                                    
  • Central PE may also present with angina-like chest pain but this is uncommon. This may reflect right ventricular (RV) ischaemia and requires differential diagnosis with acute coronary syndrome (ACS) or aortic dissection. [ESC]
    • 
                                    

   
                                 

                                    

                                    
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                    
Recurrent or transient episodes of pleuritic chest pain lasting minutes are very unlikely to be caused by multiple PEs.

                                    

                                    

   
                                 
    
                                    
  • Features of suspected DVT
                                    
      
                                    
    • Typically unilateral leg pain and swelling, although both legs may be affected.[NICEDVT]
      • 
                                    
    • Seen at presentation in 24% of patients subsequently diagnosed with a PE. [ESC]
      • 
                                    
    
                                    
    • 
                                    

                                 
                                 
Cough and hypoxaemia are also common.

                                    
    
                                    
  • Cough
                                    
      
                                    
    • Seen at presentation in 23% of patientssubsequently diagnosed with a PE. [ESC]
      • 
                                    
    
                                    
    • 
                                    
  • Hypoxaemia
                                    
      
                                    
    • Hypoxaemia at rest may be suggestive of a massive PE whereas hypoxaemia solely on exertion is more often associated with a smaller PE.
      • 
                                    
    
                                    
    • 
                                    

   
                                 

                                    

                                    
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                    
A normal oxygen saturation does not exclude PE.

                                    
    
                                    
  • Up to 40% of patients have normal arterial oxygen saturation and 20% have a normal alveolar-arterial oxygen gradient. [ESC]
    • 
                                    

                                    

                                    

   
                                 
Patients with a massive/central PE, or those with a smaller PE but who have significant pre-existing cardiorespiratory impairment, typically present with:

                                    
    
                                    
  • Syncope or pre-syncope
                                    
      
                                    
    • Seen at presentation in 6% of patients with PE. [ESC]
      • 
                                    
    
                                    
    • 
                                    
  • Hypotension
                                    
      
                                    
    • Defined as systolic blood pressure <90 mmHg. [ESC][NICECKS]
      • 
                                    
    
                                    
    • 
                                    

                                    
    
                                    
  • Other features of shock [ESC]
                                    
      
                                    
    • Look for signs of altered cognition. Agitation and distress characterise early and milder stages, whereas unresponsiveness indicates more severe and advanced shock.
      • 
                                    
    • Check for cool extremities, mottled or ashen skin, slow capillary refill, clamminess, and oliguria.
      • 
                                    
    
                                    
    • 
                                    
  • Tachycardia
                                    
      
                                    
    • Heart rate >100 bpm.
      • 
                                    
    • Tachycardia is an important sign of a possible high-risk PE even in the absence of other signs of shock.
      • 
                                    
    
                                    
    • 
                                    

   
                                 
Rarer features of PE:

                                    
    
                                    
  • Fever
                                    
      
                                    
    • Temperature >37.8°C. [BELL]
      • 
                                    
    • Present in 10% of patients with PE. [ESC]
      • 
                                    
    
                                    
    • 
                                    
  • Haemoptysis
                                    
      
                                    
    • Present in 8% of patients with PE. [ESC]
      • 
                                    
    • It is commonly estimated that around half of patients presenting with apparent haemoptysis do not have true haemoptysis. Instead this could be epistaxis or blood from the gastrointestinal tract, upper airways, or gingiva. It is vital to take a clear and thorough history and examination to distinguish this.
      • 
                                    
    
                                    
    • 
                                    

   
                                 

                                    

                                    
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                    
A PE is unlikely to be the cause in a patient who has an episode of syncope or pre-syncope but subsequently has normal observations, is haemodynamically stable and has no abnormality suggestive of a PE on their ECG.

History

                                 

                              

                              

                                 
Your history should check for the following risk factors to help assess the likelihood of PE and to determine whether any subsequently confirmed PE is provoked or unprovoked: [ESC][NICE][] [POMP] [CROLES] [ORTEL]

                                 
    
                                 
  • No provoking cause can be found in 30% of patients. [ESC]
    •

                                             Strong risk factors
                                           

                                         
                                         
                                           

                                             
                                             
                                                   
Active cancer

                                                   
    
                                                   
  • The highest relative risk of PE is in patients with multiple myeloma, brain and pancreatic cancer.
    • 
                                                   
  • The largest numbers of episodes of PE are in patients with lung, colon, and prostate cancer. [FREYZEK]
    • 
                                                   
  • Active cancer is present in 22% of patients with confirmed PE. [GOLD]
    • 
                                                   

                                                   
Recent surgery or hospitalisation (last 3 months)

                                                   
    
                                                   
  • Present in 29% of patients with confirmed PE. [GOLD]
    • 
                                                   
  • Lower limb fractures and joint replacements are strong provoking factors for PE. [ESC]
    • 
                                                   

                                                   
Previous or current DVT

                                                   
    
                                                   
  • Present in 25% of patients with confirmed PE. [GOLD]
    •

  • There is a more than fourfold increased risk of thrombosis throughout pregnancy.
    •

  • >5 days of bed rest present in 28% of patients with confirmed PE. [GOLD]

                                                   Drug history
                                                   Weak risk factors
                                               

                                             
                                             
                                               

                                                 
                                                 
                                                      
Combined oral contraceptive pill 

                                                      
    
                                                      
  • The risk of venous thromboembolism (VTE) associated with different combined oral contraceptives (COCs) is influenced by the progestogen type. COCs containing levonorgestrel, norethisterone, or norgestimate have the lowest risk (5-7 events per 10,000 women per year), whereas those containing drospirenone, desogestrel, or gestodene have the highest risk (9-12 events per 10,000 women per year). [EMA]
    • 
                                                      
  • Progestogen-only methods of contraception do not appear to be associated with an increased risk of VTE. [FAMP]
    • 
                                                      

                                                      
Hormone replacement therapy [NICE]

                                                      
    
                                                      
  • There is a small increased risk of PE or DVT associated with use of oral hormone replacement therapy (HRT) but no increased risk from transdermal HRT at standard doses. [NG23]
    • 
                                                      

                                                 		
                                               

                                             
                                       

                                       

                                             
                                               

                                                   Family history
                                                   Weak risk factors
                                               

                                             
                                             
                                               

                                                 
                                                 
                                                      
First-degree relative with a history of confirmed PE or DVT. [NICE]

                                                 		
                                               

                                             
                                       

                                 

                                 

                                    

                                    
                                    
                                    

                                       
significantly increases the risk of a PE

                                       
Evidence suggests that the overall risk of venous thromboembolism (VTE) in cancer patients is four times higher than in the general population. [HEIT] 

                                       
    
                                       
  • Although the largest absolute number of VTE episodes occur in patients with lung, colon, and prostate cancer, a population-based cohort study demonstrated that the relative risk for VTE was highest in multiple myeloma, brain, and pancreatic cancer (46-, 20- and 16fold increased risk vs. healthy controls respectively). [FREYZEK]
    • 
                                       
  • In the metastatic stage, there is evidence that stomach, bladder, uterine, renal, and lung cancer are also associated with a high incidence of VTE. [HARVEY]
    • 
                                       

                                    

                                    

                                 

                              

                              
                              

                                 

                                    
Physical examination

                                 

                              

                              

                                 
Physical examination is often non-specific unless a PE is massive/central. However, on examination you should check for:

                                    
    
                                    
  • Signs of a concurrent DVT. Examine the lower limbs for tenderness, changes to skin colour and temperature, and venous distension. [NICEDVT]
    • 
                                    
  • Hypoxaemia.  This may be present only on exertion in smaller PEs but the patient may appear tachypnoeic. [NICE]
    • 
                                    
  • Crepitations on auscultation of the lungs [NICECKS]
    • 
                                    
  • Fever. Temperature >37.8°C. [BELL]
    • 
                                    

   
                                    

                                    

                                    
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                    
It is possible for PE and pneumonia to co-exist.

                                    
    
                                    
  • Fever may be present in both but tends to be higher(>39°C) in pneumonia. [STEIN PD]
                                       
    • 
                                    

                                    
It is important to note that chest pain on palpation of the chest wall occurs in up to 20% of people with PE. 

                                    
    
                                    
  • Therefore do not use this finding to distinguish musculoskeletal chest pain from PE. [CP]
                                       
    • 
                                    

                                    

                                    

                                 
                                 
Look for features of a massive/central PE and/or severely reduced haemodynamic reserve including:

                                    
    
                                    
  • Shock or hypotension (systolic BP <90 mmHg).
    • 
                                    
  • Present in less than 5% of patients with acute PE. [LAPORTE]
    • 
                                    
  • Look for signs of altered cognition. Agitation and distress characterise early and milder stages, whereas unresponsiveness indicates more severe and advanced shock.
    • 
                                    
  • Check for cool extremities, clamminess, mottled or ashen skin, slow capillary refill, and oliguria.
    • 
                                    
  • Tachycardia (heart rate >100 bpm) is an important sign of a possible high-risk PE even in the absence of other signs of shock.
    • 
                                    
  • Signs of acute  right ventricular (RV) strain. The patient may have an elevated jugular venous pressure, sternal heave or accentuated pulmonary component of S2, although this is uncommon. [JC]
    • 
                                    

                                          
                              

                              

                                 

                                    
Assessing the clinical probability of PE

                                 

                              

                              

                                 
The symptoms and signs of PE are non-specific and no individual risk factor, patient symptom, or clinical sign can definitively diagnose or exclude PE. [ESC]

                                    
    
                                    
  • Assessment of clinical probability is therefore a key step in the diagnostic approach.
    • 
                                    

                                    
PE can be ruled out in around 30% of patients who present to the emergency department with a suspected PE through a combination of clinical probability assessment using the Wells (or Geneva) score, plus D-dimer testing if indicated. [ESC]

                                    
    
                                    
  • This probability-based approach minimises the unnecessary use of potentially harmful computed tomographic pulmonary angiography (CTPA) in patients who have a low probability of a PE. [ARN]
    • 
                                    
  • Haemodynamically stable patients are categorised as 'PE likely' or 'PE unlikely' based on their Wells (or Geneva) score.
    • 
                                    
  • Start anticoagulation and arrange CTPA for any patient in the 'PE likely' group
    • 
                                    
  • The use of the Pulmonary Embolism Rule-Out Criteria (PERC) is becoming increasingly widespread to further assess patients categorised as 'PE unlikely' based on Wells (or Geneva) score.
                                    
      
                                    
    • A PE can effectively be ruled out in any such patient who meets all the PERC criteria. [ACP]
      • 
                                    
    • Those who do not meet the PERC criteria should have a D-dimer test.
      • 
                                    
    
                                    
    • 
                                    

   
                                    
Wells score

                                    
If a non-pregnant patient with suspected PE is haemodynamically stable, use the Wells (or Geneva) score to assess the clinical probability of PE and to guide investigations. [ESC]

                                    
The two-level Wells score categorises patients as ‘PE likely’ (Wells score >4) or ‘PE unlikely’ (Wells score ≤4).[ESC][NICE]

                                    
  • A simplified version is also available, under which each criterion scores a single point. A total score of 0-1 is categorised as ‘PE unlikely’ and a score of ≥2 is categorised as ‘PE likely’.
    • 
                                    
  • An earlier three-level iteration of the Wells score (where patients are categorised into low, moderate, or high clinical probabilities of PE) has been used but the two-level score is generally preferred. [ESC]

Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                       
Wells score is commonly used in the UK but an alternative  called the Geneva score is also available. 

                                       
    
                                       
  • Choose which score to use based on  local protocols.
    • 
                                       

                                       
Do not use Wells or Geneva scores in pregnancy.

                                       
    
                                       
  • If you suspect PE in a pregnant patient, seek advice from a senior clinician.
    • 
                                       

                                       

                                       

                                          
                                    
PE likely (Wells score>4)

                                       
Give immediate anticoagulation to any patient with a Wells Score > 4 (unless contraindicated) while awaiting investigation with definitive imaging (CTPA if available). [ESC]

                                       
    
                                       
  • If anticoagulation is contraindicated these patients will require close monitoring to observe for signs of cardiorespiratory deterioration. Prioritise these patients for urgent investigation which may include an urgent echocardiography to assess RV function as well as CTPA to confirm or exclude a PE.
    • 
                                       

                                       
Do not perform D-dimer testing if a patient’s Wells score puts them in the 'PE likely' category as imaging is warranted regardless of a negative D-dimer value. [ESC] [ACP]

                                       
PE unlikely (Wells score ≤4)

                                       
If a patient has a Wells score ≤4 ('PE unlikely'), apply the Pulmonary Embolism Rule-Out Criteria (PERC) to determine whether or not further investigations are needed. [ACP]

   
                                    

                                          

                                             
                                             
                                             

                                                
Wells and Geneva scores have similar sensitivity and specificity
   
                                                
In a systematic review PE was confirmed in 12% of patients classified as 'PE unlikely' and in 50% of patients classified as 'PE likely'. [CER].

                                                   
    
                                                      
  • Neither one of Wells or Geneva score has been shown to be superior to the other. However, the Geneva score is based solely on objective clinical terms and may be more reproducible whereas Wells includes a subjective judgement of whether an alternative diagnosis is less likely than PE. [ESC]
    • 
                                                      
  • Sensitivity of the Wells and Geneva scores ranged from 88% to 96% and specificity from 48% to 53% when each clinical decision tool was validated in a primary care dataset. [HEN] 
    • 
                                                   

                                             

                                          

                                    

   
                                       
Pulmonary Embolism Rule-Out Criteria (PERC)

                                          
Apply PERC to any patient in whom a PE was initially suspected but who is classified as 'PE unlikely'using Wells (or Geneva) score. 

                                          
    
                                          
  • No further investigation is indicated if a patient meets all the PERC criteria (a PE can effectively be ruled out). The risk for PE is considered to be lower than the risk of testing. [RAJA] [ACP]
    • 
                                          
  • Request D-dimer testing for any patient in whom the PERC criteria fail to rule out a PE (i.e., one of more criterion not fulfilled).
    • 
                                          
  • The PERC criteria are:
                                          
      
                                          
    • Age <50 years
      • 
                                          
    • Heart rate <100 bpm
      • 
                                          
    • SaO₂ on room air ≥95%
      • 
                                          
    • No unilateral leg swelling
      • 
                                          
    • No haemoptysis
      • 
                                          
    • No recent surgery or trauma (≤4 weeks ago requiring treatment with general anaesthesia)
      • 
                                          
    • No prior PE or DVT
      • 
                                          
    • No hormone use (oral contraceptives, hormone replacement, or oestrogenic hormones used in male or female patients)
      • 
                                          
    
                                          
    • 
                                          

   
                                       

                                          

                                          
                                          
                                          

                                             
Use of PERC can help prevent over-investigation

                                                
A large meta-analysis of 12 studies determined that the overall proportion of PEs missed by using PERC was only 0.3% (44 of 14,844 total cases).

                                                
    
                                                
  • The pooled sensitivity of PERC across all 12 studies was 97% (95% CI, 96% to 98%), and the pooled specificity was 22% (95% CI, 22% to 23%), indicating that 22% of D-dimer tests could have been safely avoided had the PERC approach been universally applied. [SINGH]
    • 
                                                

                                          

                                          

                                          

   
                              

                              

                                 

                                    
Investigations

                                 

                              

                              

                                 
Laboratory tests

                                    
D-dimer

                                    
    
                                       .fronte
                                    
  • Request D-dimer testing in any patient whose Wells (or Geneva) score categorises them as ‘PE unlikely’ but who fails to fulfil all the PERC criteria. [RAJA] [ACP]
    • 
                                    
  • Do not use D-dimer testing in patients whose Wells (or Geneva) score categorises them as ‘PE likely’ as a negative result will not remove the need for further imaging. [RAJA] [ACP]
    • 
                                    
  • A normal plasma D-dimer level (typically <500 nanograms/mL, but check the local threshold) safely excludes PE in patients with an unlikely pre-test probability of PE and no further investigation is required. [ACP][ESC][NICE][RAJA] The risk of PE within 3 months is <1% in these patients. [CARRIER]
    • 
                                    
  • If a patient has a raised D-dimer level, start anticoagulation (unless contraindicated) and arrange CTPA to confirm or exclude PE (unless a concurrent DVT is suspected). [NICE] [ESC] [ACP]
    • 
                                    

                                    
D-dimer testing is highly sensitive (>95%) in patients in whom pre-test probability suggests that PE is unlikely. [RAJA] However, its specificity is low and may decline with increasing patient age. [RAJA]

   
                                    

                                       

                                       
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                       
Remember that a D-dimer level is a continuous variable. 
                                 
    
                                       
  • A low D-dimer does not mean no risk of PE and a slightly higher D-dimer does not mean a step increase in PE risk.
    • 
                                       
  • The threshold value (check what value is used by your local laboratory) is the optimum for balancing sensitivity and specificity.
    • 
                                       

                                       
 In practice, many people have a raised D-dimer without having a PE.
                        
    
                                             
  • It is more frequently elevated in patients with cancer, in hospitalised patients, with increasing age, with acute inflammatory conditions and infection, and during pregnancy. Take this into consideration if investigating these patient groups. [ESC] [NICE]
                                             
    • 
                                          

                                       

                                    

                                    
                                       
Full blood count

                                       
    
                                       
  • May indicate thrombocytopenia or anaemia. These patients are at an increased risk of complications from bleeding when taking an anticoagulant.
    • 
                                       
  • Heparin therapy can be associated with heparin-induced thrombocytopenia; Measure platelet count at baseline and regularly throughout treatment.
    • 
                                       
  • May also indicate thrombocythaemia or polycythaemia, which increase the risk of venous thromboembolism (VTE).
    • 
                                       

                                       
Urea and electrolytes

                                       
    
                                       
  • Check baseline renal function. Doses of some anticoagulants (e.g., low molecular weight heparin, fondaparinux, apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban) may need to be adjusted in patients with renal impairment. [NICE]
    • 
                                       
  • Renal function results can also be used to guide investigations. For example using CTPA in patients with severe renal impairment can risk contrast-induced nephropathy.
    • 
                                       

                                       
Coagulation studies

                                       
    
                                       
  • Order international normalised ratio (INR), prothrombin time (PT), and activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT). These are needed to establish baseline values before starting anticoagulation.
    • 
                                       
  • Also aids decisions about the safety and choice of initial anticoagulation. [NICE]
    • 
                                       

                                       
Liver function tests

                                       
    
                                       
  • Abnormal LFTs can influence the choice of anticoagulation.
    • 
                                       

                                       
Arterial blood gas (ABG)

                                       
    
                                       
  • Do not perform an ABG if the oxygen saturations are within normal range on room air.
    • 
                                       
  • Consider ABG in patients who have low oxygen saturations. However, be aware that it is of very limited diagnostic utility, either alone or in combination with other clinical variables, in suspected PE.[ROGER]
    • 
                                       

                                       

   
                                    

                                       

                                       
                                       
                                       

                                       
ABG has a limited role in diagnosing PE

                                          
    
                                          
  • A PaO₂ <80 mmHg (10.7 kPa), a PaCO₂ <36 mmHg (4.6 kPa), or an abnormal alveolar-arterial gradient (A–aO₂) are not predictive of PE in patients suspected of having PE. [ROGER]
    • 
                                          
  • In patients with suspected acute PE with normal arterial blood gas (ABG) results, PE could not be excluded in 38% of those without cardiopulmonary disease or in 14% of those who had pre-existing cardiopulmonary disease. [PD]
    • 
                                       

                                       

                                       

                                       

   
                                       
Cardiac biomarkers

                                       
The cardiac biomarkers high sensitivity troponin (either I or T), NT-proBNP or BNP are not used in diagnosing PE. [BTS]

                                       
    
                                       
  • They can be used in combination with risk stratification scoring (the full Pulmonary Embolism Severity Index score [PESI], or its simplified sPESI version) to aid prognostic assessment of patients with confirmed or highly suspected PE, particularly to guide decisions about early discharge for low-risk patients. [ESC]
    • 
                                       

   
                                       
Measure a cardiac biomarker in a patient with a confirmed or highly suspected PE (positive D-dimer or Wells score >4) only if all three of the following criteria are met: [BTS]

                                       
    
                                       
  • Risk stratification puts the patient in the low-risk group (PESI I-II or sPESI 0), AND
    • 
                                       
  • Incidental RV strain was identified on CTPA or echocardiography performed as part of the diagnostic work-up, AND
    • 
                                       
  • The patient is considered potentially suitable for outpatient management. [BTS]
                                       
      
                                       
    • If the cardiac biomarker is normal, you can consider the patient for discharge (after appropriate discussion with a senior). [BTS]
      • 
                                       
    • If cardiac biomarker is elevated, admit the patient for observation. [BTS]
      • 
                                       
    
                                       
    • 
                                       

                                       
Do not use routinely to risk stratify patients

                                       
Seek senior review if there is an incidental finding of an elevated troponin in a patient with a low-risk PE (PESI I or II/sPESI 0)

                                       
    
                                       
  • An alternative cause for elevated troponin should be considered. [BTS]
    • 
                                       

   
                                       
For more detail on risk stratification, see the Management - Recommendations section.

   
                                       

                                             

                                             
                                             
                                             

                                             
Elevated cardiac biomarkers predict a worse outcome

                                             
Elevated plasma troponin levels on admission have been reported to be associated with a worse prognosis.
 
                                                
    
                                                
  • A meta-analysis covering a total of 1985 patients showed elevated cardiac troponin I or T concentrations in approximately 50% of the patients with acute PE. Elevated troponin concentrations were associated with higher mortality both in unselected patients and haemodynamically stable patients. [BECATTINI]
                                                   
    • 
                                                

                                                
Low levels of BNP or NT-proBNP can identify patients with a favourable short-term outcome based on their high negative predictive value. [COUTANCE]

                                                
    
                                                
  • The plasma levels of BNP and NT-proBNP reflect the severity of haemodynamic compromise and (presumed) RV dysfunction in acute PE. RV pressure overload is associated with increased myocardial stretch, which leads to the release of BNP or NT-proBNP and therefore raised plasma levels. [ESC]
    • 
                                                
  • A meta-analysis found that 51% of 1132 unselected patients with acute PE had elevated BNP or NT-proBNP concentrations on admission. These patients had a 10% risk of early death and a 23% risk of an adverse outcome.[HENZLER]
    • 
                                             

                                             

                                             

                                       

   
                                             
Imaging

                                             
Chest x-ray (CXR)

                                               
                                             
 A CXR is not diagnostic of PE but may be useful to support the diagnosis and is important to rule out other causes.

                                             
    
                                                
  • It is usually normal in a patient with a PE but an abnormal CXR does not rule out PE.
    • 
                                                
  • In a patient with PE, it may show: [NICE]          
                                                
      
                                                   
    • Atelectasis
      • 
                                                   
    • Pleural effusion
      • 
                                                   
    • Elevation of a
      • 
                                                   
    • hemidiaphragm
      • 
                                                
    
                                                
    • 
                                             

   
                                             

                                                

                                                
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                                
Pulmonary infarction secondary to PE can be difficult to distinguish from pneumonia on CXR.

                                                

                                             

                                             
                                             
ECG

                                             
An ECG is not diagnostic of PE but can be useful to support the diagnosis of PE or rule out other causes.

                                             
    
                                                
  • A normal ECG does not rule out a PE. [NICE]
    • 
                                             

                                             
ECG signs that may be present in a patient with PE include:[ESC][ACP] [DIGBY] [BAJAJ]

                                             
    
                                                
  • Normal sinus rhythm
    • 
                                                
  • Sinus tachycardia
    • 
                                                
  • New right bundle branch block (complete or incomplete)
    • 
                                                
  • QR pattern in V1
    • 
                                                
  • S1Q3T3 pattern
    • 
                                                
  • T wave inversion in V1-V4
    • 
                                                
  • ST segment migration in V1 through V4
    • 
                                                
  • Atrial arrhythmias, most frequently atrial fibrillation
    • 
                                                
  • Non-specific ST-segment and T-wave abnormalities
    • 
                                                
  • Right axis deviation
    • 
                                                
  • P-pulmonale
    • 
                                                

   
                                                

                                                   

                                                   
                                                   
                                                   

                                                   
Some ECG signs are predictors of a poor outcome

                                                      
    
                                                      
  • A systematic review and meta-analysis looked at the value of using ECG in predicting clinical deterioration and mortality in acute PE.
    • 
                                                      
  • It found that ECG signs that were good predictors of a poor outcome included S1Q3T3, complete right bundle branch block, T-wave inversion, right axis deviation, and atrial fibrillation. [DIGBY]
    • 
                                                      

                                                   

                                                   

                                                

   
                                             
Computed tomographic pulmonary angiography (CTPA)

                                             
    
                                             
  • CTPA is the preferred investigation for definitive confirmation of PE
      
                                             
    • Diagnosis is confirmed by direct visualisation of a thrombus in a pulmonary artery, which appears as a partial or complete intraluminal filling defect.
      • 
                                             
    •                                           
                                             
  • CTPA is indicated in:
                                             
      
                                             
    • Patients with suspected PE who are haemodynamically unstable at presentation (provided CTPA is immediately available and the patient is well enough to have it - in practice this is often not the case and echocardiography is used instead).
      • 
                                             
    • Patients who are classified as ‘PE likely’ based on Wells (or Geneva) score.
      • 
                                             
    • Patients who are classified as ‘PE unlikely’ based on Wells or Geneva score but who have a positive D-dimer test. [NICE]
      • 
                                             
    • 
                                             
  • Avoid CTPA if possible in pregnant women and younger patients, especially if PE can be ruled out by other non-invasive methods with less radiation exposure. [ARN]
    • 
                                             
  • PE does not need to be confirmed on CTPA prior to discharge in low-risk patients (PESI I-II or sPESI 0) who are suitable for outpatient management but it should be performed within 24 hours (if available). [BTS]
    • 
                                             

                                             
                                             

                                                

                                                
                                                
                                                

                                                   
CTPA must be used judiciously to ensure benefits outweigh risks

                                                   
Radiation from CT is considered to be a risk factor for cancer.

                                                   
    
                                                   
  • In one study, a cohort of children with CT exposure was found to have a significantly higher incidence of leukaemia and brain tumours later in life. Women in particular are also at significant risk due to the imaging of radiosensitive breast tissue during CTPA. [ACP]
    • 
                                                   

                                                   
The contrast dye used in CTPA for the evaluation of PE may cause nephropathy.

                                                   
    
                                                   
  • In a prospective study, 174 patients underwent CTPA, which demonstrated acute PE in 7%. Some 12% developed contrast-induced nephropathy including one patient with acute PE. Development of contrast-induced nephropathy was associated with an increased risk of severe renal failure and death within the subsequent 45 days.
    • 
                                                   

                                                   
Repeated imaging is common, which may magnify these risks.

                                                   
    
                                                   
  • In one study performed at a large academic centre, at least one third of emergency department patients who had CTPA for the evaluation of PE underwent another CT for the same reason within 5 years. [KLINE]
    • 
                                                   

                                                   
More widespread use of CTPA may lead to overdiagnosis of subsegmental PEs that are often of little clinical significance. 

                                                   
    
                                                   
  • The diagnostic value and clinical significance of subsegmental defects identified on CTPA remains hotly debated. [ESC take-home messages doc].
    • 
                                                   
  • Evidence suggests that there has been minimal or no associated change in patient outcomes despite the increased use of CTPA. It is thought that this is due to increased identification of less severe PEs (such as subsegmental PEs) that would not have previously been diagnosed and that may not be clinically relevant. [BOONE] [BRODER] [SOJ]
    • 
                                                   
  • There is a lack of evidence to support decisions on whether or not to treat patients who have incidental PE detected by CTPA, although they probably should be treated if they have cancer and a proximal clot. [TH]
    • 
                                                   
  • Potentially inappropriate overuse of CTPA results in increased risks to patients as well as higher costs.[ACP]
    • 
                                                   

                                                

                                                

                                             

                                             
                                             

                                                

                                                
                                                
                                                

                                                   
The predictive value of CTPA depends on the pre-test probability of PE

                                                   
The PIOPED II trial highlighted the influence of clinical probability on the predictive value of CTPA. [PIOPED]

                                                   
    
                                                   
  • The negative predictive value for PE of a negative CTPA was dependent on pre-test clinical probability of PE as assessed by the Wells score (96% for those with a low probability pre-test Wells score and 89% for those with an intermediate probability score, but only 60% for those with a high probability Wells score - based on the three-level Wells Score)
    • 
                                                   
  • Conversely the positive predictive value of a positive CTPA was high (92%-96%) in patients with an intermediate or high probability pre-test Wells score but only 58% in those with a low probability pre-test score.
    • 
                                                   

                                                   
Therefore, clinicians should be particularly cautious in case of discrepancy between clinical judgement and the CTPA result. However a normal CTPA excludes a PE in a patient whose pre-test Wells (or Geneva) score categorised them as PE unlikely. [ESC]

                                                

                                                

                                             

                                             
                                             
Echocardiography

                                             
    
                                             
  • Use echocardiography as an alternative diagnostic investigation for haemodynamically unstable patients with suspected PE who cannot have CTPA.
                                             
      
                                             
    • If CTPA is not immediately available or the patient is too unwell to be moved, perform a bedside echocardiography.
      • 
                                             
    • The presence of any signs of RV dysfunction is sufficiently strongly suggestive of PE to justify emergency reperfusion treatment (usually thrombolysis). [ESC]
      • 
                                             
    • 
                                             
  • Echocardiography can also identify important alternative causes of shock, such as pericardial tamponade, acute valvular dysfunction, severe global or regional LV dysfunction, aortic dissection, or hypovolaemia.
    • 
                                             
  • Do not use echocardiography routinely in haemodynamically stable patients with suspected PE. 
    • 
                                             
  • Findings in PE include: [ESC]
                                             
      
                                             
    • Abnormal RV ejection pattern (so-called ‘60–60 sign’)
      • 
                                             
    • Reduced contractility of the RV free wall compared with the RV apex (‘McConnell sign’)[ESC]
      • 
                                             
    • RV dilatation and hypokinesis
      • 
                                             
    • RV diameter/LV diameter >0.9
      • 
                                             
    • Interventricular septal flattening and paradoxical leftward septal motion
      • 
                                             
    • Presence of tricuspid regurgitation
      • 
                                             
    • Presence of pulmonary hypertension (peak tricuspid jet velocity >2.6 m/s and loss of respirophasic IVC collapse)
      • 
                                             
    • 
                                             
  • Mobile right heart thrombi can be detected by transthoracic or transoesophageal echocardiography (or by CT angiography) in less than 4% of unselected patients with PE. This finding essentially confirms the diagnosis of PE and is associated with RV dysfunction and high early mortality. [ESC]
    • 
                                             

   
                                             
   
                                             

                                                

                                                
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                                

                                                   
A negative echocardiography does not exclude PE. [ESC]

                                                
    
                                                
  • The reported negative predictive value for PE is 40% to 50%.
    • 
                                                

                                                
Assessment of RV function on echocardiography is not obligatory for the identification of low-risk patients who are suitable for outpatient management. [BTS]

                                                
    
                                                
      
                                                
    • However, if it has been performed as part of the diagnostic work-up for PE and shows evidence of RV dysfunction, consider measuring a cardiac biomarker to further risk stratify the patient.
      • 
                                                
    
                                                

                                                

                                             

   
                                             
Lower limb compression venous ultrasound

                                             
    
                                             
  • Use lower limb compression venous ultrasound toinvestigate patients suspected of having a concurrent DVT.
                                             
      
                                             
    • Finding a proximal DVT in patients suspected of having PE is considered sufficient to start anticoagulant treatment without further investigation. [ESC]
      • 
                                             
    • 
                                             
 
   
                                             
                                             

                                                   

                                                   
                                                   
                                                   

                                                         
Lower limb ultrasound has high sensitivity/specificity for DVT

                                                         
  • Lower limb compression venous ultrasound has a sensitivity >90% and a specificity of approximately 95% for symptomatic DVT and shows a DVT in 30% to 50% of patients with PE. [ESC]

                                                   

                                                   

                                                

   
                                                
Ventilation/perfusion (V/Q) scan

                                                
    
                                                
  • A V/Q scan can be performed in patients with an elevated D-dimer and a contraindication to CTPA (such as pregnancy, renal impairment, or contrast allergy). [ESC] [ACP] [NICE]
    • 
                                                
  • V/Q may be preferred over CTPA in some patient groups to avoid unnecessary radiation, particularly in younger and female patients in whom CTPA may raise the lifetime risk of cancer. [ESC] [ACP]
    • 
                                                

   
                                                

                                                      

                                                      
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                                      
A V/Q scan is of limited use in patients with significant underlying lung disease, left ventricular failure, or congestive cardiac failure. [ESC]
                                                         

                                                      

                                                   

   
                                                                                            
                                             

                                                   

                                                   
                                                   
                                                   

                                                      
Evidence supports the potential use of V/Q scanning as the first-line imaging test for PE in younger patients

                                                      
    
                                                            
  • V/Q lung scintigraphy has been shown to be diagnostic (with either normal or high-probability findings) in approximately 30% to 50% of emergency ward patients with suspected PE. The proportion of diagnostic V/Q scans was higher in patients with a normal chest x-ray. [DIDIER]
    • 
                                                            
  • The number of patients with inconclusive findings on V/Q scan may be reduced by taking into account clinical probability. Evidence shows that patients with a negative lung scan and low clinical probability of PE have a low prevalence of confirmed PE. The negative predictive value of this combination is further increased by the absence of a DVT on lower limb compression venous ultrasound. [PIOPE90][SORSH]
    • 
                                                            

                                                   

                                                   

                                                

   
   
                              

                              

                                 

                                    
Suspected PE in pregnancy

                                 

                              

                              

                        
                              
PE is the leading cause of maternal mortality in developed countries.[ESC] [TOWER]

                              
    
                              
  • There is a fourfold increased risk of VTE throughout pregnancy compared with non-pregnant women and this is relatively equal across all three trimesters.
    • 
                              
  • This risk rises sharply in the postnatal period and is up to 60 times higher in the 3 months after delivery, compared with the risk in non-pregnant women.
    • 
                              

                              
Symptoms and signs of PE may be less specific in pregnant women. [ACP]

                              
    
                              
  • Be cautious in interpreting breathlessness as it is a common symptom in pregnancy. [ESC]
    • 
                              

                              
If you suspect PE in a pregnant patient, seek advice from a senior clinician.

                              
    
                              
  • Do not use the Wells (or Geneva) score in pregnancy - they are not reliable predictors of the probability of PE.
    • 
                              

                              
Minimise exposure to radiation-associated imaging when investigating suspected PE in a pregnant woman. 

                              
    
                              
  • Use lower limb compression ultrasound if concurrent DVT is suspected. Some centres now advocate bilateral lower limb ultrasound even if there are no clinical signs of DVT.
    • 
                              
  • There is debate over whether V/Q scan or CTPA is preferred for the next step.
                              
      
                              
    • The European Society of Cardiology and the American Thoracic Society consensus guidelines recommend a V/Q scan if the patient has had a normal CXR, with a CTPA if the V/Q scan is inconclusive. [LEUNG] [ESC]
      • 
                              
    • In practice the decision between perfusion scanning and CTPA will depend on local equipment and expertise, as well as patient factors (normal CXR, ability to hold breath). [ACR]
      • 
                              
    • Halve the administered dose of radiopharmaceutical when lung scans are indicated in pregnant women; longer acquisition times should be used to achieve adequate imaging. [STRAITON]
      • 
                              
    • 
                              

   
                                            
                              

                                    

                                    
                                    
                                    

                                          
Data is lacking on the validity of clinical probability scores in pregnancy

                                          
    
                                          
  • A recent retrospective series of 125 pregnant women who were referred for CTPA showed that no patient with an initial Wells score <6 had a PE. [CONNOR]
    • 
                                          
  • These results need to be confirmed in large-scale prospective studies. In the meantime these scoring tools should not be used.
    • 
                                          

                                    

                                    

                                 

   
                                 

                                       

                                       
                                       
                                       

                                             
The use of D-dimer in pregnancy remains controversial

                                             
A normal D-dimer result has the same exclusion value for PE in pregnant women as for other patients with suspected PE but is found more rarely.
 
                                             
    
                                                   
  • This is because plasma D-dimer levels rise physiologically throughout pregnancy. [CHABLOZ]
    • 
                                                   
  • A study of a series of pregnant patients with suspected DVT found that an agglutination assay would have ruled out the diagnosis in 55% of the cases with a negative predictive value of 100%. [CHAN]The same study attempted to establish higher cut-off levels in pregnancy for several commonly used D-dimer assays.
    • 
                                                   
  • These thresholds await prospective validation. In the meantime, check local guidance on whether to use D-dimer in pregnancy and what cut-off value should apply to rule out PE in pregnancy. [ESC]
    • 
                                                   

                                       

                                       

                                    

   
   
                              

   
                              

                                 

                                    
Further assessment and investigation of unprovoked PE

                                 

                              

                              

                                   
                                 
An unprovoked PE is a PE in a patient who had no pre-existing, transient provoking risk factor in the prior 3 months. [NICE]

                                 
    
                                 
  • Provoking risk factors include: surgery; trauma; significant immobility (bedbound, unable to walk unaided, or likely to spend a substantial proportion of the day in bed or in a chair); pregnancy or puerperium; use of oral contraceptive/hormone replacement therapy).
    • 
                                 

                                 
An unprovoked PE may be suggestive of an underlying condition so further investigations are sometimes warranted.

   
                                 
Undiagnosed cancer

   
                                 
Offer any patient diagnosed with an unprovoked PE who is not already known to have cancer the following initial investigations: [NICE]

                                 
    
                                 
  • A physical examination (guided by the patient’s full history)
    • 
                                 
  • Chest x-ray
    • 
                                 
  • Blood tests (full blood count, serum calcium, and liver function tests)
    • 
                                 
  • Urinalysis
    • 
                                 

                                 
Consult local guidelines when deciding whether to undertake additional investigations for cancer. 

                                 
    
                                 
  • NICE recommends considering a CT scan of the abdomen and pelvis (and a mammogram for women) in any patient over 40 years with a first unprovoked PE who does not have signs or symptoms of cancer based on initial investigation. [NICE]
    • 
                                 
  • However, in clinical practice it is common to investigate further only if there are signs or symptoms of cancer in the history or examination or abnormalities picked up on initial investigations. Further investigation with CT scan or mammogram can be considered but the sensitivity is low and many local guidelines do not recommend these tests.
    • 
                                 

   
                                                                          
                                 

                                       

                                       
                                       
                                       

                                          
The evidence supporting screening for undiagnosed cancer after unprovoked VTE is inconclusive. 

                                          
    
                                                
  • A screening strategy that was proposed in the SOMIT trial included pelvic and abdominal CT combined with mammography and sputum cytology. This was recommended as the most effective and least harmful approach for patients.
    • 
                                                
  • However, no 5-year survival benefit was found when this approach was compared with basic clinical evaluation. [PRINS][DOORMAAL]
    • 
                                                

                                       

                                       

                                    

   
                                    
Thrombophilia testing[NICE]

                                    
Consider testing for hereditary thrombophilia in a patient who has had an unprovoked PE and who has a first-degree relative who has had a DVT or PE if it is planned to stop anticoagulation treatment.

                                    
    
                                    
  • Do not offer thrombophilia testing to patients who are continuing anticoagulation treatment.
    • 
                                    

                                    
Consider testing for antiphospholipid antibodies in patients who have had an unprovoked PE if it is planned to stop anticoagulation treatment.

                                    

Do not routinely offer thrombophilia testing to first-degree relatives of people with a history of PE and thrombophilia.

   
                              

                              

                              

                                 
Emerging/future investigations

                              

                           

                           

                                 
Age-adjusted D-dimer testing

                                 
    
                                 
  • Recent evidence suggests using age-adjusted cut-offs can improve the performance of D-dimer testing in patients aged over 50 years. [ACP][ESC][SCHOUTEN]
    • 
                                 
  • D-dimer specificity is low and may decline with increasing patient age. [RAJA]
    • 
                                 

   
                                                                          
                                 

                                       

                                       
                                       
                                       

                                             
Age-adjusted D-dimer thresholds might increase specificity

                                             
    
                                             
  • A recent meta-analysis of 13 studies and 12,497 patients without high pre-test clinical probability of PE found that the use of age-adjusted D-dimer cut-offs for patients aged over 50 years (age × 10 ng/mL) maintained a sensitivity for PE above 97% while significantly increasing specificity. [SCHOUTEN]
    • 
                                             
  • In patients with cancer, using an age-adjusted D-dimer cut-off doubled the proportion of patients in whom PE could be excluded by clinical decision rule and D-dimer, without the need for imaging. [WILTS]
    • 
                                             

                                       

                                       

                                    

   
                                    
Magnetic resonance angiography (MRA)

                                    
    
                                    
  • The hypothesis is that a negative MRA combined with the absence of proximal DVT on lower limb compression ultrasound may safely rule out clinically significant PE [ESC]
    • 
                                    
      
                                    
    • This is being tested in a multicentre outcome study.
      • 
                                    
    
                                    

                                    
MRA has shown promise but is not yet ready for clinical practice due to its low sensitivity, high proportion of inconclusive scans, and low availability in most emergency settings.
