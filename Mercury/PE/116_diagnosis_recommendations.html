Recommendations

         

            

               

                  

                     
Urgent considerations

                     
    
                        
  • Assess for signs of shock or hypotension (ABCDE approach) as this can indicate a massive/central PE with high risk of a poor outcome. 
                        
      
                        
    • Seek senior support and escalate to critical care if a high-risk PE is suspected. [ESC][ACP]
      • 
                        
    • A high-risk PE is defined as a systolic blood pressure (SBP) <90 mmHg or an SBP drop ≥ 40 mmHg for >15 minutes, if not caused by a new-onset arrhythmia, hypovolaemia, or sepsis. [ESC]
      • 
                        
    • Other signs of shock include altered cognition, cool extremities, mottled or ashen skin, slow capillary refill, clamminess, and oliguria.
      • 
                        
    • If the patient is in peri-arrest/cardiac arrest and PE is suspected it is common practice to give thrombolysis without waiting for results of investigations, based on discussion between senior clinicians.
      • 
                        
    • Ensure early respiratory support. Titrate oxygen to 94% to 98% (or 88% to 92% for patients at risk of hypercapnic failure). [ESC]
      • 
                        
    • Give intravenous fluids if SBP < 90 mmHg or there are other signs of shock but monitor for signs of heart failure. [ESC]
      • 
                        
    
                        
    • 
                        
  • In a patient with shock or hypotension, urgently confirm or rule out a PE using either computed tomographic pulmonary angiography (CTPA) or echocardiography (if CTPA is unavailable or the patient’s condition is so critical that only bedside investigations are possible). [ESC]
                        
      
                        
    • If PE is confirmed on CTPA or signs of right ventricular (RV) overload are seen on echocardiography, arrange urgent primary reperfusion, usually with thrombolysis.
      • 
                        
    
                        
    • 
                        

                  

               

            


            
Key points

               

                     

                        

                           
Presentation

                           
    
                              
  • Suspect pulmonary embolism (PE) in a patient with acute onset of dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, or features of deep vein thrombosis (DVT).
                              
      
                              
    • At least one of these features is present in 97% of people with PE. [MISS]
      • 
                              
    • Symptoms typically come on suddenly rather than gradually.
      • 
                              
    • Dyspnoea is the most common presenting feature and may be acute and severe in a central PE but is often mild and transient in a small, peripheral PE.
      • 
                              
    
                              
    • 
                              
  • Around 95% of patients with PE are haemodynamically stable on presentation.
                                 
      
                              
    • The 5% of patients who have a high-risk (haemodynamically unstable) PE may also have features of hypotension, shock, or tachycardia and can be hypoxaemic at rest.
      • 
                              
    • 
                              
                              
  • A patient with a PE may also present with cough or, more rarely, with fever or haemoptysis. [ESC]
    • 
                              
  • Strong risk factors include pregnancy/6 weeks postpartum; active cancer; recent surgery or immobilisation; and a history of DVT. [ESC]
    • 
                           

                                 
                            

                           
Assessing the clinical probability of PE

                           
    
                              
  • Use the Wells (or Geneva) score to assess the likelihood of a PE in any patient who is haemodynamically stable. This will categorise the patient into either ‘PE likely’ (Wells score >4) or 'PE unlikely' (Wells score ≤4).[ESC] [NICE] 
    • 
                              
  • PE can be ruled out in around 30% of patients who present to the emergency department with a suspected PE through a combination of clinical probability assessment using the Wells (or Geneva) score, plus D-dimer testing if indicated. [ESC]
    • 
                              
  • If a patient has a ‘PE likely’ Wells (or Geneva) score, start anticoagulation then arrange investigation with definitive imaging (computed tomographic pulmonary angiography [CTPA] if available).
    • 
                              
  • If a patient has a 'PE unlikely' Wells (or Geneva) score, order a D-dimer unless the patient fulfils all the Pulmonary Embolism Rule-Out Criteria (in which case a PE can be effectively ruled out and no further investigations are indicated). [NICE] [ESC] [ACP]
    • 
                              
  • If D-dimer level is elevated, start anticoagulation and arrange CTPA to confirm or exclude a PE. A normal D-dimer level safely excludes a PE in a patient who had a 'PE unlikely' Wells (or Geneva) pre-test score.
    • 
                              
  • Other differentials to consider include respiratory, cardiac, and musculoskeletal causes as well as gastro-oesophageal reflux disease and pregnancy. Other causes of syncope or pre-syncope should also be examined including vasovagal syncope, orthostatic (postural) hypotension, cardiac arrhythmias, seizures, and cerebrovascular disorders. [ESC]
    • 
                           

                           
                           

                           
Diagnostic confirmation

                           
    
                              
  • Confirm diagnosis in most patients using CTPA, which will show direct visualisation of a thrombus in a pulmonary artery and appears as a partial or complete intraluminal filling defect. [ESC] [NICE][ACP]
    • 
                              
  • Avoid CTPA radiation in patients with a suspected concurrent DVT by using lower limb compression venous ultrasound. [NICE] [ACP]
    • 
                              
  • A ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) scan may be preferred to CTPA to minimise radiation exposure in certain patient groups. This is a radiation- and contrast medium-sparing procedure and should be used in patients with contraindications or relative contraindications to CT (e.g, contrast allergy, moderate to severe renal failure, pregnancy, young patients).[ESC] [NICE]
    • 
                           

                           

                           
Suspected PE in pregnancy

                           
    
                              
  • PE is the leading cause of maternal mortality in developed countries. There is a fourfold increased risk of venous thromboembolism (VTE) throughout pregnancy compared with non-pregnant women. This risk rises sharply in the postnatal period and is up to 60 times higher in the 3 months after delivery, compared with the risk in non-pregnant women. [ESC] [TOWER]
    • 
                              
  • Symptoms and signs of PE may be less specific in pregnant women. [ACP] Be cautious in interpreting breathlessness as it is a common symptom in pregnancy. [ESC]
    • 
                              
  • Investigation of PE in pregnancy should aim to minimise exposure to radiation-associated imaging wherever possible. [ESC]
    •

Clinical presentation

                              

                           

                           

                              
Symptoms of PE typically have an acute rather than a gradual onset [ESC].

                              
Patients typically present with at least one of dyspnoea, pleuritic chest pain, or features of deep vein thrombosis (DVT). [ESC] [NICE]

                              
    
                              
  • Dyspnoea
                              
      
                              
    • Themost common presenting feature - it is present in 50% of patientswho are subsequently found to have a PE. [ESC]
      • 
                              
    • Dyspnoea may be acute and severe in a central PE but is often mild and may be transient in a small peripheral PE. [ESC]
      • 
                              
    • Tachypnoea (respiratory rate ≥20 breaths per minute) is a common presenting sign in patients with acute PE (21%-39%). [POLLACK]
      • 
                              
    
                              
    • 
                              


                              

                                 

                                 
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                 
In a patient with pre-existing heart failure or lung disease, worsening dyspnoea may be the only presenting symptom of a PE.[ESC]

                                 

                                 

                                 
Pleuritic chest pain


                                 
    
                                 
  • Seen at presentation in 39% of patientssubsequently diagnosed with a PE. [ESC]
    • 
                                 
  • The pain is normally localised to one side of the chest and is unlikely to be central. This is usually caused by pleural irritation due to distal emboli causing pulmonary infarction. [STEIN]
    • 
                                 
  • Central PE may also present with angina-like chest pain but this is uncommon. This may reflect right ventricular (RV) ischaemia and requires differential diagnosis with acute coronary syndrome (ACS) or aortic dissection. [ESC]
    • 
                                 


                              

                                 

                                 
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                 
Recurrent or transient episodes of pleuritic chest pain lasting minutes are very unlikely to be caused by multiple PEs.

                                 

                                 


                              
    
                                 
  • Features of suspected DVT
                                 
      
                                 
    • Typically unilateral leg pain and swelling, although both legs may be affected.[NICEDVT]
      • 
                                 
    • Seen at presentation in 24% of patients subsequently diagnosed with a PE. [ESC]
      • 
                                 
    
                                 
    • 
                                 

                              
                              
Cough and hypoxaemia are also common.

                                 
    
                                 
  • Cough
                                 
      
                                 
    • Seen at presentation in 23% of patientssubsequently diagnosed with a PE. [ESC]
      • 
                                 
    
                                 
    • 
                                 
  • Hypoxaemia
                                 
      
                                 
    • Hypoxaemia at rest may be suggestive of a massive PE whereas hypoxaemia solely on exertion is more often associated with a smaller PE.
      • 
                                 
    
                                 
    • 
                                 


                              

                                 

                                 
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                 
A normal oxygen saturation does not exclude PE.

                                 
    
                                 
  • Up to 40% of patients have normal arterial oxygen saturation and 20% have a normal alveolar-arterial oxygen gradient. [ESC]
    • 
                                 

                                 

                                 


                              
Patients with a massive/central PE, or those with a smaller PE but who have significant pre-existing cardiorespiratory impairment, typically present with:

                                 
    
                                 
  • Syncope or pre-syncope
                                 
      
                                 
    • Seen at presentation in 6% of patients with PE. [ESC]
      • 
                                 
    
                                 
    • 
                                 
  • Hypotension
                                 
      
                                 
    • Defined as systolic blood pressure <90 mmHg. [ESC][NICECKS]
      • 
                                 
    
                                 
    • 
                                 

                                 
    
                                 
  • Other features of shock [ESC]
                                 
      
                                 
    • Look for signs of altered cognition. Agitation and distress characterise early and milder stages, whereas unresponsiveness indicates more severe and advanced shock.
      • 
                                 
    • Check for cool extremities, mottled or ashen skin, slow capillary refill, clamminess, and oliguria.
      • 
                                 
    
                                 
    • 
                                 
  • Tachycardia
                                 
      
                                 
    • Heart rate >100 bpm.
      • 
                                 
    • Tachycardia is an important sign of a possible high-risk PE even in the absence of other signs of shock.
      • 
                                 
    
                                 
    • 
                                 


                              
Rarer features of PE:

                                 
    
                                 
  • Fever
                                 
      
                                 
    • Temperature >37.8°C. [BELL]
      • 
                                 
    • Present in 10% of patients with PE. [ESC]
      • 
                                 
    
                                 
    • 
                                 
  • Haemoptysis
                                 
      
                                 
    • Present in 8% of patients with PE. [ESC]
      • 
                                 
    • It is commonly estimated that around half of patients presenting with apparent haemoptysis do not have true haemoptysis. Instead this could be epistaxis or blood from the gastrointestinal tract, upper airways, or gingiva. It is vital to take a clear and thorough history and examination to distinguish this.
      • 
                                 
    
                                 
    • 
                                 


                              

                                 

                                 
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                 
A PE is unlikely to be the cause in a patient who has an episode of syncope or pre-syncope but subsequently has normal observations, is haemodynamically stable and has no abnormality suggestive of a PE on their ECG.

Evidence suggests that the overall risk of venous thromboembolism (VTE) in cancer patients is four times higher than in the general population. [HEIT] 

                                                
    
                                                
  • Although the largest absolute number of VTE episodes occur in patients with lung, colon and prostate cancer, a population-based cohort study demonstrated that the relative risk for VTE was highest in multiple myeloma, brain and pancreatic cancer (46-, 20- and 16 fold increased risk vs. healthy controls respectively.) [FREYZEK]
    • 
                                                
  • In the metastatic stage, there is evidence that stomach, bladder, uterine, renal and lung cancer are also associated with a high incidence of VTE. [HARVEY]

Physical Examination

                              

                           

                           

                              
Physical examination is often non-specific unless a PE is massive/central. However, on examination you should check for:

                                 
    
                                 
  • Signs of a concurrent DVT. Examine the lower limbs for tenderness, changes to skin colour and temperature, and venous distension. [NICEDVT]
    • 
                                 
  • Hypoxaemia. This may be present only on exertion in smaller PEs but the patient may appear tachypnoeic. [NICE]
    • 
                                 
  • Crepitations on auscultation of the lungs [NICECKS]
    • 
                                 
  • Fever. Temperature >37.8°C. [BELL]
    • 
                                 


                                 

                                 

                                 
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                 
It is possible for PE and pneumonia to co-exist.

                                 
    
                                 
  • Fever may be present in both but tends to be higher(>39°C) in pneumonia. [STEIN PD]
                                    
    • 
                                 

                                 
It is important to note that chest pain on palpation of the chest wall occurs in up to 20% of people with PE. 

                                 
    
                                 
  • Therefore do not use this finding to distinguish musculoskeletal chest pain from PE. [CP]
                                    
    • 
                                 

                                 

                                 

                              
                              
Look for features of a massive/central PE and/or severely reduced haemodynamic reserve including:

                                 
    
                                 
  • Shock or hypotension (systolic BP <90).
    • 
                                 
  • Present in less than 5% of patients with acute PE. [LAPORTE]
    • 
                                 
  • Look for signs of altered cognition. Agitation and distress characterise early and milder stages, whereas unresponsiveness indicates more severe and advanced shock.
    • 
                                 
  • Check for cool extremities, clamminess, mottled or ashen skin, slow capillary refill and oliguria.
    • 
                                 
  • Tachycardia (heart rate >100bpm) is an important sign of a possible high-risk PE even in the absence of other signs of shock.
    • 
                                 
  • Signs of acute RV strain. The patient may have an elevated jugular venous pressure, sternal heave or accentuated pulmonary component of S2, although this is uncommon. [JC]
    • 
                                 

                                       
                           

                           

                              

                                 
Assessing the clinical probability of PE

                              

                           

                           

                              
The symptoms and signs of PE are non-specific and no individual risk factor, patient symptom, or clinical sign can definitively diagnose or exclude PE. [ESC]

                                 
    
                                 
  • Assessment of clinical probability is therefore a key step in the diagnostic approach.
    • 
                                 

                                 
PE can be ruled out in around 30% of patients who present to the emergency department with a suspected PE through a combination of clinical probability assessment using the Wells (or Geneva) score, plus D-dimer testing if indicated. [ESC]. 

                                 
    
                                 
  • This probability-based approach minimises the unnecessary use of potentially harmful computed tomographic angiography (CTPA) in patients who have a low probability of a PE. [ARN]
    • 
                                 
  • Haemodynamically stable patients are categorised as 'PE likely' or 'PE unlikely' based on their Wells (or Geneva) score.
    • 
                                 
  • Start anticoagulation and arrange CTPA for any patient in the 'PE likely' group
    • 
                                 
  • The use of the Pulmonary Embolism Rule-Out Criteria (PERC) is becoming increasingly widespread to further assess patients categorised as 'PE unlikely' based on Wells (or Geneva) score.
                                 
      
                                 
    • A PE can effectively be ruled out in any such patient who meets all the PERC criteria. [ACP]
      • 
                                 
    • Those who do not meet the PERC criteria should have a D-dimer test
      •

Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                    
Wells Score is commonly used in the UK but an alternative  called the Geneva score is also available. 

                                    
    
                                    
  • Choose which score to use based on  local protocols.
    • 
                                    

                                    
Do not use Wells or Geneva Scores in pregnancy.

                                    
    
                                    
  • If you suspect PE in a pregnant patient, seek advice from a senior clinician.
    • 
                                    

                                    

                                    

                                       
                                 
PE likely (Wells Score>4)

                                    
Give immediate anticoagulation to any patient with a Wells Score > 4 (unless contraindicated) while awaiting investigation with definitive imaging ( CTPA if available). [ESC]

                                    
    
                                    
  • If anticoagulation is contraindicated these patients will require close monitoring to observe for signs of cardiorespiratory deterioration. Prioritise these patients for urgent investigation which may include an urgent echocardiography to assess right ventricular function as well as CTPA to confirm or exclude a PE.
    • 
                                    

                                    
Do not perform D-dimer testing if a patient’s Wells Score puts them in the 'PE likely' category as imaging is warranted regardless of a negative D-dimer value. [ESC] [ACP]

                                    
PE unlikely (Wells Score ≤4)

                                    
If a patient has a Wells Score ≤4 ('PE unlikely'), apply the pulmonary embolism rule-out criteria (PERC) to determine whether or not further investigations are needed.  [ACP]


                                 

                                       

                                       
                                       
                                       

                                             
In a systematic review PE was confirmed in 12% of patients classified as 'PE unlikely' and in 50% of patients classified as 'PE likely' [CER].

                                             
    
                                             
  • Neither one of Wells or Geneva Score has been shown to be superior to the other. However, the Geneva score is based solely on objective clinical terms and may be more reproducible whereas Wells includes a subjective judgement of whether an alternative diagnosis is less likely than PE. [ESC]
    • 
                                             
  • Sensitivity of the Wells and Geneva Scores ranged from 88% to 96% and specificity from 48% to 53% when each clinical decision tool was validated in a primary care dataset. [HEN] 
    • 
                                             

                                       

                                       

                                       


                                    
Pulmonary embolism rule out criteria (PERC)

                                       
Apply PERC to any patient in whom a PE was initially suspected but who is classified as 'PE unlikely'using Wells (or Geneva) Score. 

                                       
    
                                       
  • No further investigation is indicated if a patient meets all the PERC criteria (a PE can effectively be ruled out). The risk for PE is considered to be lower than the risk of testing. [RAJA] [ACP]
    • 
                                       
  • Request D-dimer testing for any patient in whom the PERC criteria fail to rule out a PE (i.e. one of more criterion not fulfilled).
    • 
                                       
  • The PERC criteria are:
                                       
      
                                       
    • Age <50
      • 
                                       
    • HR <100 bpm
      • 
                                       
    • SaO₂ on room air ≥95%
      • 
                                       
    • No unilateral leg swelling
      • 
                                       
    • No haemoptysis
      • 
                                       
    • No recent surgery or trauma (≤4 weeks ago requiring treatment with general anesthesia)
      • 
                                       
    • No prior PE or DVT
      • 
                                       
    • No hormone use (oral contraceptives, hormone replacement or oestrogenic hormones use in male or female patients)
      • 
                                       
    
                                       
    • 
                                       


                                    

                                       

                                       
                                       
                                       

                                             
A large meta-analysis of 12 studies determined that the overall proportion of PEs missed by using PERC was only 0.3% (44 of 14,844 total cases) .

                                             
    
                                             
  • The pooled sensitivity of PERC across all 12 studies was 97% (95% CI, 96% to 98%), and the pooled specificity was 22% (CI, 22% to 23%), indicating that 22% of D-dimer tests could have been safely avoided had the PERC approach been universally applied. [SINGH]
    • 
                                             

                                       

                                       

                                       


                           

                           

                              

                                 
Investigations

                              

                           

                           

                              
Laboratory tests

                                 
D-dimer

                                 
    
                                 
  • Request D-dimer testing in any patient whose Wells (or Geneva) Score categorises them as ‘PE unlikely’ but who fails to fulfil all the PERC criteria. [RAJA] [ACP]
    • 
                                 
  • Do not use D-dimer testing in patients whose Wells (or Geneva) Score categorises them as ‘PE likely’  as a negative result will not remove the need for further imaging. [RAJA] [ACP]
    • 
                                 
  • A normal plasma D-dimer level  (typically <500 nanograms/mL but check the local threshold) safely excludes PE in patients with an unlikely pretest probability of PE and no further investigation is required. [ACP][ESC][NICE][RAJA] The risk of PE within 3 months is <1% in these patients. [CARRIER]
    • 
                                 
  • If a patient has a raised D-dimer level, start anticoagulation (unless contraindicated) and arrange CTPA to confirm or exclude PE (unless a concurrent DVT is suspected). [NICE] [ESC] [ACP]
    • 
                                 

                                 
D-dimer testing is highly sensitive (>95%) in patients in whom pre-test probability suggests that PE is unlikely. [RAJA] However, its specificity is low and may decline with increasing patient age. [RAJA]


                                 

                                    

                                    
Practical tip / Pitfall panel

                                    
Remember that a D-dimer level is a continuous variable. 
                                 
    
                                    
  • A low D-dimer does not mean no risk of PE and a slightly higher D-dimer does not mean a step increase in PE risk.
    • 
                                    
  • The threshold value (check what value is used by your local laboratory) is the optimum for balancing sensitivity and specificity.
    • 
                                    

                                    
 In practice, many people have a raised D-dimer without having a PE.
                        
    
                                          
  • It is more frequently elevated in patients with cancer, in hospitalised patients, with increasing age, with acute inflammatory conditions and infection and during pregnancy. This should be taken into consideration if investigating these patient groups. [ESC] [NICE]
                                          
    • 
                                       

                                    

                                    

                                 
                                    
Full blood count

                                    
    
                                    
  • May indicate thrombocytopenia or anaemia. These patients are at an increased risk of complications from bleeding when taking an anticoagulant.
    • 
                                    
  • Heparin therapy can be associated with heparin-induced thrombocytopenia; platelet count should be measured at baseline and regularly throughout treatment.
    • 
                                    
  • May also indicate thrombocythaemia or polycythaemia, which increase the risk of VTE.
    • 
                                    

                                    
Urea and electrolytes

                                    
    
                                    
  • Check baseline renal function. Doses of some anticoagulants (e.g. low molecular weight heparin, fondaparinux, apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran, edoxaban) may need to be adjusted in patients with renal impairment. [NICE]
    • 
                                    
  • Renal function results can also be used to guide investigations. For example using CTPA in patients with severe renal impairment can risk contrast-induced nephropathy.
    • 
                                    

                                    
Coagulation studies

                                    
    
                                    
  • Order International normalised ratio (INR), prothrombin time (PT), and activated partial thromboplastin time (aPTT). These are needed to establish baseline values before starting anticoagulation.
    • 
                                    
  • Also aids decisions about the safety and choice of initial anticoagulation. [NICE]
    • 
                                    

                                    
Liver function tests

                                    
    
                                    
  • Abnormal LFTs can influence the choice of anticoagulation.
    • 
                                    

                                    
Arterial blood gas (ABG)

                                    
    
                                    
  • Do not perform an ABG if the oxygen saturations are within normal range on room air.
    • 
                                    

                                    
Consider ABG in patients who have low oxygen saturations. However, be aware that it is of very limited diagnostic utility, either alone or in combination with other clinical variables, in suspected PE.[ROGER]


                                 

                                    

                                    
                                    
                                    

                                    
    
                                       
  • A PaO₂ <80 mmHg (10.7 kPa), a PaCO₂ <36 mmHg (4.6 kPa), or an abnormal alveolar-arterial gradient (A–aO₂) are not predictive of PE in patients suspected of having PE. [ROGER]
    • 
                                       
  • In patients with suspected acute PE with normal arterial blood gas (ABG) results, PE could not be excluded in 38% of those without cardiopulmonary disease or in 14% who had pre-existing cardiopulmonary disease. [PD]
    • 
                                    

                                    

                                    

                                    


                                    
    Cardiac biomarkers

                                    
The cardiac biomarkers high sensitivity troponin (either I or T), NT-proBNP or BNP are not used in diagnosing PE. [BTS]

                                    
    
                                    
  • They can be used in combination with risk stratification scoring (PESI/sPSEI) to aid prognostic assessment of patients with confirmed or highly suspected PE, particularly to guide decisions about early discharge for low-risk patients. [ESC]
    • 
                                    

                                    
Measure a cardiac biomarker in a patient with a confirmed or highly suspected PE (positive D-dimer or Wells score >4) only if all three of the following criteria are met: [BTS]

                                    
    
                                    
  • risk stratification puts the patient in the low-risk group (PESI I-II or sPESI 0) AND
    • 
                                    
  • incidental RV strain was identified on CTPA or echocardiography performed as part of the diagnostic work-up AND
    • 
                                    
  • the patient is considered potentially suitable for outpatient management [BTS]
                                    
      
                                    
    • If the cardiac biomarker is normal, you can consider the patient for discharge (after appropriate discussion with a senior). [BTS]
      • 
                                    
    • If cardiac biomarker is elevated, admit the patient for observation. [BTS]
      • 
                                    
    
                                    
    • 
                                    

                                    
Do not use routinely to risk stratify patients

                                    
Seek senior review if there is an incidental finding of an elevated troponin in a patient with a low-risk PE (PESI I or II/sPESI 0)

                                    
    
                                    
  • An alternative cause for elevated troponin should be considered. [BTS]
    • 
                                    


                                    
For more detail on risk stratification, see the Management - Recommendations section.
